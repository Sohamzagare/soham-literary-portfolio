In My Own Words

I was never supposed tolove words this much.

I am a second-year AIML engineering student at PCCOE, Pune — and somewhere between debugging algorithms and reading literature, I realised something: they are the same thing.

I chose English Literature as my multidisciplinary subject because I believe the two worlds I live in are not opposites. When I write code, I am narrating a problem. When I write fiction, I am computing the human condition. Both are attempts to make the world legible — to find pattern inside chaos, to say: here, this is how it works.

My essay, Literature Meets Technology, came from a question I kept asking myself in class: if a medieval poet could see a poem go viral on Instagram, what would they think? I wrote about how Wi-Fi does what they thought was magic, and why — despite algorithms, recommendation engines, and trending feeds — the human need for an honest story remains the one thing no system can optimise away.

My novel, The Exit Row, is something more personal. It is about a broken pilot named Daniel and a sharp girl named Lia on a plane that doesn't reach Toronto. Seven days in below-zero wilderness. Gunshots. A sabotaged crash. And between all of that — the slow, quiet, impossible work of learning to trust someone you didn't plan to meet.

I wrote it because I needed to understand what it costs to leave the people who love you, and what it costs — even harder — to go back to them.

Literature MeetsTechnology

On how Wi-Fi does what medieval poets thought was magic — and why the human need for story is the one thing no algorithm can optimise away.

AuthorSoham B. Zagare
CourseEnglish Literature · PCCOE
ProgrammeSE AIML · 2025
From manuscript to megabyte — the medium changes, the message endures

If you had told a medieval poet that one day their words could travel across continents in less than a second, they might have assumed you were describing magic. In reality, you were simply describing Wi-Fi.

For most of history, literature depended on slow and fragile technologies — handwritten manuscripts, printing presses, libraries that could burn down, and letters that took months to arrive. Today, however, stories travel through servers, fiber-optic cables, and cloud storage. A poem written in a dorm room at midnight can reach thousands of readers before the writer has even finished their coffee.

In this sense, technology has not replaced literature. It has simply given it faster legs.

The Ancient Art of Going Viral

What is fascinating is that the internet has unintentionally recreated something very ancient. Long before books existed, literature was social and conversational. Stories were shared aloud, debated, retold, and reshaped by communities. In the digital age, literature has returned to that collective space. Readers comment, reinterpret, remix, and even collaborate with writers in real time. Platforms designed for entertainment have become unexpected literary ecosystems where micro-poetry, reflective essays, and serialized fiction circulate with astonishing speed.

"Every generation inherits old stories, debugs them, rewrites them, and releases a slightly improved version to the world."

The Engineer as Storyteller

Even engineers — who often claim to prefer logic over metaphor — are now participating in storytelling whether they realise it or not. The language of technology itself is deeply narrative. Programmers speak about architectures, frameworks, and debugging stories as if software were a living organism with moods and habits. Anyone who has spent three hours trying to fix a bug knows that coding sometimes feels less like engineering and more like detective fiction.

And if literature is the art of understanding human complexity, then technology has simply introduced new settings for the same old questions. Instead of asking how letters travel between lovers, modern stories ask how relationships survive through screens and notifications.

The logic of emotion — code as literature's quiet cousin

Algorithms as Silent Editors

Some writers even argue that algorithms themselves have become silent editors of modern literature. Recommendation systems suggest what we read, trending posts determine which poems go viral, and digital platforms decide which voices rise above the noise. Yet even within this algorithmic environment, literature continues to behave unpredictably. The stories that resonate most deeply rarely follow formulas. They emerge from authenticity, from emotional clarity, from the timeless human need to understand ourselves.

The writer Marshall McLuhan famously suggested that the medium is the message — meaning that the technologies we use to communicate inevitably shape the way we think. Yet its essence remains unchanged: a carefully arranged sequence of words capable of altering how a reader sees the world.

The Longest Open-Source Project

Technology is constantly iterated, updated, and optimised. Literature works in the same way. Every generation inherits old stories, debugs them, rewrites them, and releases a slightly improved version to the world.

You could say literature operates like the longest open-source project in human history. And unlike most software, this project has been running continuously for over three thousand years — with no sign of shutting down.

Part One
Altitude
Flight 782 Chicago O'Hare → Toronto Pearson Seat 17F · The Exit Row

Airports are honest places. That's what nobody tells you. They make you stand in lines. They take your belt, your shoes, your dignity. They show you, plainly and without sentiment, that you are one of ten thousand people in transit — not special, not exempted, not protected by rank or whatever story you'd been telling yourself on the drive over.

Daniel West had not been inside an airport in three years. Not since the day the world had decided, with the confident cruelty of the majority, what kind of man he was. Thirty-eight years old and feeling sixty — he folded himself into seat 17F, the exit row, with the efficient economy of a person who has repeated this motion so many times it no longer requires thought.

Part Two
The World After Falling

Cold does not announce itself. It is absence — the withdrawal of warmth, patient and systemic and completely indifferent to the fact that you need what it is taking. Over seven days in below-zero wilderness, two strangers learn each other the way crisis teaches: quickly, without artifice, with the particular honesty that comes when performance is simply too expensive.

After

The helicopter finds them on a frozen stream. Lia tilts her face up to the rotor wash, backpack still on her shoulders from pure habit. This is the moment of arrival — not just from the wilderness, but from the long detour both of them have been taking from the people who needed them.

A park in Boston, Saturday afternoon, May — three years, and then this
Author's Note

The Exit Row is a work of fiction. This book is about two things that are really one thing: what it costs to leave the people you love, and what it costs to go back to them. The wilderness is real. The cold is real. The shots fired are real. But the heart of it is two people in adjacent seats on a plane that never quite gets where it was going, finding each other anyway.

Thank you for coming along.
